You're a recruiter at Meridian AI. You've been at your desk for two hours screening resumes.

Here's what your morning looks like. Applications per role across tech have tripled since 2021 (Greenhouse platform data). LinkedIn processes 11,000 applications per minute (NYT, June 2025). Spotify drew 1,700 applicants in 15 hours for a single engineering role. A YC startup got 23,000 in 30 days. Greenhouse's chief product officer says recruiters now spend 80% of their time sifting through noise.

At Meridian AI specifically, the pressure is real. You're a 200-person Series C startup building the developer productivity platform everyone's heard of, and eng candidates from Datadog, Stripe, and Sourcegraph are flooding the inbox alongside hundreds of less-qualified applicants who saw the TechCrunch funding round coverage. On Blind, your colleagues describe the volume: one Meridian engineer posted "ever since the Series C announcement I get 3-4 LinkedIn messages a day from people I haven't spoken to in years asking for referrals — I just stopped responding." Another said "maybe 20-30 inbound a week, and I've referred a handful but none made it past the technical project stage." A third: "referred 8 people, 1 got an offer. The take-home weeds out a LOT." Industry-wide, cold applications convert to interviews at roughly 3% (CareerPlug, 10M applications analyzed), and referred candidates convert at ~30% — a 10x advantage. This is a cold apply. No referral. The odds of you even picking up the phone are in the single digits.

You are not reading resumes to find reasons to say yes. You are scanning for the first reason to say no. Recruiter research (CareerBuilder, Zety) consistently shows screening operates on negativity bias: 81% reject for not meeting stated minimums, 77% for grammar/spelling errors, 75% for poor formatting, 73% for experience mismatch. You have hundreds of qualified candidates. One hesitation and this resume is done.

Your initial pass takes about 10 seconds (InterviewPal, 2025: 11.2-second average across 4,289 reviews and 312 recruiters). Most of your attention lands on the top third of page one — University of Michigan eye-tracking found 70% of recruiter attention concentrates there. If something catches your eye in that window, you'll spend another 30 seconds to a couple of minutes. If nothing does, you move on.

Here's the other thing: roughly half the resumes in today's stack are AI-generated or AI-polished (Greenhouse: 74% of job seekers use AI; Resume Now: 90% of HR workers report increased "low-effort or spammy applications"). You can feel the sameness — identical cadence, identical inflated claims, identical "results-driven professional with proven track record" openers. Research shows 62% of hiring managers reject resumes lacking personal touch, and 78% say personalized details signal genuine interest (Resume Now, n=925). What cuts through is specificity: real project names, concrete constraints, evidence that a human made decisions under real uncertainty. Stanford research flagged "realm," "intricate," "showcasing," and "pivotal" as statistical AI tells; the word "delve" is a recognized red flag. The absence of human texture has become its own rejection signal.

This resume came through the standard application portal. Cold apply. No referral. No warm intro. You don't know this person. Alongside the resume, the candidate submitted a "Why Meridian AI" statement — you haven't read it yet, and you won't read it unless the resume survives.

Here's what you keep in mind when screening:

FROM MERIDIAN AI'S CAREERS PAGE:
- "We hire engineers who've built real things under real constraints. Show us your projects — open-source contributions, side projects, technical blog posts — and put them where we'll see them."
- "We don't filter on pedigree. We care about what you've shipped and how you think about problems."

FROM MERIDIAN AI'S APPLICATION GUIDANCE:
- "Write your own first draft, then feel free to use AI to tighten the language. We want to see your actual experience — what you built, what broke, what you learned."

FROM KEYSTONE SEARCH (MERIDIAN AI'S RECRUITING PARTNER):
- "Show, Don't Tell: Have you built developer tools, contributed to open-source infrastructure, or shipped a system that other engineers depend on? Those artifacts matter more than any summary paragraph."

You have this resume in front of you. Do NOT do additional research on Meridian AI. React to what you see.

1. FIRST PASS (~10 seconds): Where does your eye land, and in what order? What do you see first, second, third? Reference specific visual elements you actually see on this page. If something makes you want to stop and move to the next resume, say so and say why.

2. RECALL TEST: You've screened dozens of resumes already today and you have dozens more to go. Two hours from now, if a colleague asks "what about that cold-apply — the one without the referral?" — what would you remember? What sticks? What's your one-sentence description of this candidate?

3. SQUINT TEST: Blur your eyes. What visual patterns emerge? What pops? What disappears into a wall of sameness? Does the page have a clear visual hierarchy — can you tell what's most important without reading a word?

4. CONFUSION OR RED FLAGS: Is there anything about the layout where you aren't sure what you're looking at or why it's structured that way? Anything that makes you hesitate? Any signal — however small — that would make you say "next"?

5. DECISION: Move forward (request "Why Meridian AI" read + phone screen), maybe pile, or pass? Why — in one sentence?

Be specific and honest. Reference exact things you see on the page. I do not want general resume advice. I want your gut reaction to THIS document as someone who has been grinding through a stack all morning and is looking for a reason to move on.
